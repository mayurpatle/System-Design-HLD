# KIP-101: Alter Replication Protocol to use Leader Epoch rather than High Watermark for Truncation — Detailed Summary

**Source:** Apache Kafka wiki (cwiki.apache.org), authored primarily by Ben Stopford with Jun Rao et al., adopted in **Kafka 0.11.0.0 (2017)**. One of the most important correctness fixes in Kafka's history — it introduced the **leader epoch** machinery that everything from replication to KRaft now depends on.

---

## 1. Background: how replication worked before, and why truncation exists

Kafka replication has two phases: a follower **fetches** messages from the leader, and on its *next* fetch request it implicitly confirms receipt, which lets the leader advance the **High Watermark (HW)** — the highest offset replicated to the entire ISR. The leader then piggybacks the new HW back to followers on fetch responses.

The subtle flaw: **a follower's HW always lags the leader's by at least one round of RPC.** The leader learns "everyone has m2" first; the follower only learns it on the next response.

When a replica restarts or a new leader is elected, a replica must make sure its log doesn't contain entries the new leader doesn't have. The pre-KIP-101 rule was blunt: **on becoming a follower, truncate your log back to your own High Watermark**, then re-fetch from the leader. Because the follower's HW is stale, this truncation could throw away perfectly good *committed* data — and worse, what got re-fetched afterward could silently differ.

## 2. The two failure scenarios (the heart of the KIP)

### Scenario 1 — Data loss on fast follower restart + leader failover

Two brokers, B is leader, A is follower, `min.insync.replicas` satisfied:

1. A fetches message **m2** from B and appends it. A's HW is still behind (it hasn't received the response advancing it).
2. A **restarts**. Following the old rule, it truncates to its (stale) HW — **deleting m2**.
3. Before A can re-fetch, **B fails**. A is elected leader.
4. m2 — which was *committed* and acknowledged to the producer under `acks=all` — is **permanently lost**, whether or not B returns (B, on returning as follower, will truncate to match A).

Essence: the one-RPC lag between leader HW and follower HW, combined with truncate-to-HW, means a fast restart + failover window destroys committed data.

### Scenario 2 — Log divergence after simultaneous failure (e.g., power outage)

Kafka doesn't fsync every message (by design — durability comes from replication), so on a hard crash a replica can lose its **unflushed tail**:

1. Both A (follower) and B (leader) crash at once (rack power loss). Both had message m2 at offset 1, but neither had flushed it.
2. **A recovers first** — its unflushed m2 is gone — and becomes leader. It accepts a *new* message **m3, written at offset 1**.
3. B comes back as follower. Its offset 1 = m2; the leader's offset 1 = m3. B's HW equals A's, so **no truncation is triggered**. Fetching proceeds "normally."
4. Result: two replicas with **the same offsets holding different messages** — silent divergence. The replica is corrupt while appearing healthy; downstream this can surface as inconsistencies or runtime failures (in practice this class of bug caused replicas that could never reconcile and even message-corruption incidents).

Essence: **an offset alone does not identify a message across leadership changes.** Two different "histories" can produce the same offset.

## 3. The fix: Leader Epochs

### Key concept: Leader Epoch

A **leader epoch** is a 32-bit, monotonically increasing number, incremented by the controller **every time partition leadership changes**. From KIP-101 onward:

- **Every message batch is stamped with the epoch of the leader that accepted it** (this required the message-format change delivered alongside KIP-32/KIP-98 era formats).
- Each replica maintains a **leader-epoch checkpoint file** (`leader-epoch-checkpoint` in the partition's log directory): a simple list of `(epoch, startOffset)` pairs — "epoch E began at offset X." This is effectively a compressed **lineage/history of leadership** for the partition.

So a log entry is no longer identified by offset alone but by **(epoch, offset)** — conceptually identical to Raft's (term, index). Two histories can share an offset, but not an (epoch, offset) pair.

### Key concept: OffsetsForLeaderEpoch request (epoch fencing at truncation time)

Truncate-to-HW is replaced by **ask-the-leader**:

1. On becoming a follower (or restarting), the replica does **not** truncate to its HW. Instead it sends the new leader an **OffsetsForLeaderEpoch** request containing its own latest epoch.
2. The leader replies with the **Leader Epoch Start Offset of the epoch after the follower's epoch** — i.e., "the first offset where your history and mine diverge" — or its **Log End Offset** if the follower's epoch is still the current one.
3. The follower **truncates exactly at that offset** (only removing entries that genuinely never happened in the leader's history) and resumes fetching.

### How this fixes both scenarios

- **Scenario 1:** A restarts and, instead of truncating to HW, asks leader B for the end of its epoch. B answers with its LEO (epoch unchanged), so A truncates *nothing* — m2 survives. If B then dies and A leads, m2 is still there. **No committed data lost.**
- **Scenario 2:** B returns and queries A (now leader in a **newer epoch** that began at offset 1). A answers "your epoch ended at offset 1," so B truncates offset 1 (its phantom m2), then fetches m3. **Logs converge; divergence impossible.**

### Supporting details

- The checkpoint file is plain text with (epoch, offset) pairs; epochs also survive log compaction because compaction preserves at least one record's epoch information and the checkpoint file itself.
- **Backward compatibility:** the OffsetsForLeaderEpoch request is only used when all brokers support it (inter-broker protocol ≥ 0.11); otherwise the old HW-truncation path is used. Standard two-phase broker upgrade (bump inter-broker protocol first, message format after).
- **Rejected simpler fixes:** waiting for followers to advance HW before the leader does (adds a full RPC of latency to every produce) or deferring truncation until the first fetch returns (doesn't fix divergence). Epochs solve both cleanly.

## 4. What KIP-101 did *not* fully close — the follow-ups

- **KIP-279 (Kafka 2.0):** an edge case remained under *very fast back-to-back leader changes* (and unclean election): a follower's *latest* epoch might itself be stale in a way one round-trip can't detect. Fix: the OffsetsForLeaderEpoch exchange became iterative and the response now returns **both the end offset and the epoch**, letting the follower walk back through its epoch history until the histories genuinely match.
- **KIP-320:** extended epoch fencing to **consumers** — fetch requests carry the leader epoch so a consumer can detect it's reading from a stale leader ("fencing reads"), and offset commits store the epoch so seeks are validated after unclean elections.
- **KIP-595 (KRaft):** Kafka's Raft implementation for the metadata quorum explicitly reuses this machinery — its log reconciliation "is similar to the logic that followers use today through the OffsetsForLeaderEpoch API." KIP-101 is the reason Kafka's data-plane replication and Raft look like cousins.

## 5. Important concepts to retain (highlight reel)

- **High Watermark (HW):** highest offset replicated to all ISR members; consumers only see below it. **Propagates to followers one RPC late** — that lag is the root cause of both bugs.
- **Truncation:** on leadership change/restart, a replica must discard log entries the new leader's history doesn't contain. Pre-KIP-101: truncate to own HW (lossy, blunt). Post: truncate to the leader-supplied epoch boundary (precise).
- **Leader Epoch:** monotonically increasing generation number per leadership change; stamped into every batch; (epoch, offset) uniquely identifies a log position across histories — Kafka's equivalent of Raft's (term, index).
- **Leader Epoch Start Offset / checkpoint file:** per-replica record of where each epoch began — the partition's leadership lineage.
- **OffsetsForLeaderEpoch:** the follower→leader "where does my history diverge from yours?" request that replaces blind truncation.
- **Fencing:** the general pattern — a stale actor (old leader, restarted follower, zombie producer, stale consumer) is detected and rejected because its epoch/generation is old. Kafka uses this same pattern for producers (transactions), consumers (KIP-320), static group members, and KRaft.
- **Why fsync isn't the answer:** Kafka deliberately tolerates losing unflushed data on crash (fsync-per-write costs 2–3 orders of magnitude in throughput); the protocol must therefore reconcile logs correctly after crashes — which is exactly what epochs enable.

## 6. Real-world use cases — where this actually bites and where the idea is used

**Where the bug manifested in production (pre-0.11 clusters):**

- **Rolling restarts / broker bounces during deploys:** Scenario 1 is literally "a follower restarted at the wrong moment and then the leader died." Ops teams doing routine rolling upgrades on busy clusters occasionally lost acknowledged messages — catastrophic for payment/order events produced with `acks=all`.
- **Rack or AZ power failures:** Scenario 2 is the classic data-center power-loss case — both replicas lose unflushed tails, recover, and silently diverge. Symptoms were replicas that never rejoined ISR, checksum/corruption errors, or consumers on different replicas seeing different data at the same offset.
- Any environment with **aggressive autoscaling / spot instances / Kubernetes pod churn**, where broker restarts and leader elections are frequent, multiplies exposure to both windows.

**Where you rely on it today (mostly invisibly):**

- **Financial/event-sourced systems using `acks=all` + `min.insync.replicas=2`:** the durability contract you quote to auditors ("an acknowledged event is never lost as long as one ISR replica survives") is only actually true because truncation is epoch-based. In an OrderFlow/payments-style pipeline, KIP-101 is why a broker bounce during a deploy can't eat an acknowledged `payment.captured` event.
- **CDC / changelog topics feeding caches, search indexes, and Kafka Streams state stores:** divergence would mean two consumers rebuilding *different* states from "the same" topic. Epochs guarantee all replicas converge on one history, so derived stores stay consistent.
- **Consumer correctness after failovers (via KIP-320):** committed offsets carry epochs, so a consumer that committed against a leader that later got fenced won't silently jump to a wrong position after an unclean election.
- **KRaft metadata quorum:** the controller's own Raft log uses the same epoch/divergence-check machinery — so cluster metadata correctness itself descends from KIP-101.
- **The design pattern beyond Kafka:** "generation/epoch numbers to fence stale participants and identify log positions as (term, index)" is the same idea as Raft terms, ZooKeeper's zxid epochs, and ViewStamped Replication's view numbers. KIP-101 is the case study of what happens when a replicated log *lacks* that concept — a great interview story for "tell me about a subtle distributed-systems bug."

## 7. TL;DR

Pre-0.11 Kafka truncated follower logs to the High Watermark on restart/failover. Because a follower's HW lags the leader's by one RPC, this could (1) delete committed messages ahead of an ill-timed leader failure, and (2) allow two replicas to hold *different messages at the same offsets* after a simultaneous crash, since offsets alone can't distinguish histories. KIP-101 stamps every batch with the **leader epoch**, checkpoints each epoch's start offset per replica, and replaces truncate-to-HW with an **OffsetsForLeaderEpoch** exchange: the follower asks the leader where their histories diverge and truncates precisely there. Result: committed data survives failovers, replicas provably converge, and Kafka gains the (epoch, offset) ≙ Raft (term, index) foundation later reused by consumer fencing (KIP-320), divergence hardening (KIP-279), and KRaft itself (KIP-595).
