# KIP-279: Fix Log Divergence Between Leader and Follower After Fast Leader Failover — Detailed Summary

**Source:** Apache Kafka wiki (cwiki.apache.org), authored by Anna Povzner. **Accepted; shipped in Kafka 2.0 (2018).** Direct follow-up to KIP-101: even with leader-epoch-based truncation, two edge cases could still make leader and follower logs diverge. KIP-279 closes them for **both clean and unclean leader election**.

---

## 1. Context: what KIP-101 left open

KIP-101 replaced truncate-to-High-Watermark with an epoch exchange: on becoming a follower, a replica sends **OffsetsForLeaderEpoch(myLatestEpoch)** and the leader replies with one end offset, which the follower truncates to.

The hidden assumption: **one round trip is enough** — i.e., the leader always recognizes the follower's latest epoch, or the answer it gives is still safe to act on. Under *fast, back-to-back leader changes*, that assumption breaks: the follower's latest epoch may be one the **leader has never heard of**, and the leader's reply (computed against a *different* epoch) can be misinterpreted.

The one-line diagnosis from the KIP: *the follower receives an offset for an epoch it doesn't know about, and acts by comparing it with offsets from a different epoch.* What we actually want is to truncate to the **largest common log prefix — where both epoch AND offset match**.

## 2. The two divergence scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Preferred leader election followed by an immediate second failover (CLEAN election!)

This is the alarming one, because it needs no unclean config and no data-losing crash — just ordinary leadership churn:

1. **Epoch 1:** A leads. A appends batches [0–10] and [11–20]. Only [0–10] replicates to B. Both are in ISR.
2. **Epoch 2:** B becomes leader via **preferred leader election** (A stays in ISR — no one left the ISR, that's the trap). B appends its own batch [11–n].
3. Before A (now follower) manages to truncate, **B's ZooKeeper session expires** — A becomes leader again with **epoch 3** and appends [21–30].
4. B, becoming follower, sends OffsetsForLeaderEpoch(epoch 2) to A. **A never had epoch 2**, so (per KIP-101 behavior) it answers with the start offset of its first epoch greater than 2 → **offset 21**.
5. Now B compares an epoch-3-boundary offset against its epoch-2 log, with three outcomes depending on n:
   - **n < 20:** B truncates nothing and fetches from n → **out-of-order offset error at runtime** (A returns the full batch from 11, which B can't append).
   - **n == 20:** B truncates nothing, fetches from 21, and everything *looks* fine — but offsets 11–20 hold **different messages on A and B**: **silent divergence**.
   - **n > 20:** B truncates to 21 (mid-batch → back to 10), refetches from 11 — accidentally fine.

So the same bug produces either a runtime replication failure or, worse, undetected divergence.

### Scenario 2 — Fast alternating failovers with unclean leader election

RF=2, min.isr=1, brokers alternate as sole survivor (this is Appendix (a) of KIP-101, which KIP-101 explicitly didn't fix):

- Epoch 0: write to A (offset 0), stop A; B leads.
- Epoch 1: write to B (offset 0), stop B; A leads.
- Epoch 2: write to A (offset 1), stop A; B leads.
- Epoch 3: write to B (offset 1). Bring A back.
- A asks B about epoch 2. B only knows epochs 1 and 3, so it replies with the first offset of epoch 3 (= 1). But **offset 0 is already divergent** (A's epoch-0 message vs B's epoch-1 message). B can tell *something* is wrong — but a single answer **can't tell where the divergence started**.

## 3. The fix: return the epoch too, and walk back until histories meet

Two changes, one idea:

**Protocol change (public interface):** `OffsetsForLeaderEpochResponse` V1 adds a **`leader_epoch` field** next to `end_offset`. The leader now answers: *"the largest epoch ≤ the one you asked about is E, and E ends at offset X."* The reply is no longer an ambiguous bare offset — it says **which epoch the offset belongs to**.

**Follower algorithm (the epoch walk-back loop):**

1. Follower sends OffsetsForLeaderEpoch with its latest epoch (same as KIP-101).
2. Leader replies with (largest epoch ≤ requested, end offset of that epoch).
3. If the follower **doesn't** have that epoch in its leader-epoch checkpoint file: truncate to the end of its own largest epoch below the returned one, and **ask again with that smaller epoch**. Repeat — walking backwards through epoch history — until the leader returns an epoch the follower actually has.
4. Once a **common epoch** is found: truncate away all local offsets with larger epochs, then truncate to the leader's returned end offset if it's smaller than the local LEO.
5. Resume normal fetching.

**Convergence target: largest common epoch first, then largest common offset within it** — i.e., the true largest common log prefix. In principle the two replicas can compare their **entire epoch lineage**; in practice it converges in one round trip almost always.

**Round-trip cost:** clean elections are still guaranteed **one round trip** (you can't get two back-to-back preferred elections without ISR changes, so the follower has at most one epoch unknown to the leader — and the new epoch field resolves it immediately). Unclean elections usually take one; ≥3 consecutive fast failovers may need two.

**Fallback / compatibility:** if the leader can't resolve an epoch at all (pre-epoch data, or history exhausted), it returns `UNKNOWN_OFFSET_FOR_LEADER_EPOCH` and the follower falls back to KIP-101-style truncate-to-HW (also the behavior on any request error/timeout). Rollout is the standard two-step inter-broker-protocol bump.

**Worked resolution of Scenario 1:** B asks about epoch 2 → A replies **{epoch 1, offset 21}** → B *has* epoch 1, so it truncates everything above epoch 1 (its [11–n] batch, LEO=11); since 21 > 11 it just fetches from 11. One round trip, no divergence, for all values of n.

**Worked resolution of Scenario 2:** A asks about epoch 2 → B replies {epoch 1, offset 1} → A doesn't have epoch 1... walks back to epoch 0 → B exhausts its history → `UNKNOWN_OFFSET_FOR_LEADER_EPOCH` → A truncates to its HW (offset 0) and refetches. Divergence repaired (data loss is inherent to unclean election, but the logs now converge).

## 4. Interesting supporting details

- **Compaction caveat:** the correctness argument needs the leader-epoch checkpoint file to preserve history. If the file is lost and must be rebuilt from a *compacted* log where every record of some epoch was compacted away, entries go missing. The KIP notes (without implementing) two fixes: leave an epoch tombstone when compacting away an epoch's last records, or never compact past the persisted HW.
- **Rejected alternative 1** — same walk-back but via an error code instead of adding the epoch field: rejected because it costs an extra round trip even in clean elections; the chosen design buys the one-round-trip guarantee for 32 extra bits per partition.
- **Rejected alternative 2** — follower ships its whole (epoch, end offset) sequence between HW and LEO and the leader computes the divergence point: fewer round trips in exotic unclean cases, but bigger requests and more complexity for scenarios that are already rare.
- **Rejected clean-only fixes:** persist HW on every leader change (fsync overhead not worth it), or kick the old leader out of the ISR on preferred election (doesn't help unclean cases).

## 5. Important concepts to retain (highlight reel)

- **Log divergence:** leader and follower holding *different records at the same offsets*. The failure can be loud (out-of-order fetch errors) or **silent** (n == 20 case) — silent is worse.
- **Largest common log prefix:** the correct truncation point is defined by **(epoch, offset) both matching**, never by offset alone. KIP-279 is the KIP that makes Kafka actually find it.
- **Epoch lineage comparison / walk-back:** divergence detection is inherently **iterative** — you may need to walk the leadership history backwards until the two replicas agree on an epoch. One answer can prove *something* diverged; only the walk finds *where*.
- **Unambiguous responses:** the fix's essence is adding one field so a reply is self-describing — "(offset X *of epoch E*)" instead of a bare offset the receiver might interpret against the wrong epoch. A general protocol-design lesson.
- **Preferred leader election as a trigger:** leadership can bounce with **no broker failing and no ISR change** (rebalancing leadership is routine!), which is exactly why "follower has an epoch the leader never saw" happens in healthy clusters.
- **Raft parallel:** this is the same log-reconciliation problem Raft solves with its AppendEntries consistency check walking `nextIndex` backwards until (term, index) match. KIP-279 brings Kafka's data plane to parity, and KIP-595's KRaft fetch protocol explicitly reuses this divergence-check style.
- **Guarantee lattice:** KIP-101 fixed truncation *when leader and follower share the latest epoch*; KIP-279 fixes it *whatever their histories are* — clean and unclean.

## 6. Real-world use cases — where this matters

**Situations that trigger the bug (why the fix protects you):**

- **Routine leadership rebalancing on busy clusters:** preferred leader election runs automatically (`auto.leader.rebalance.enable`) or during rolling restarts/deploys; combine it with one ZooKeeper session hiccup (GC pause, network blip) and you have Scenario 1 exactly — in a perfectly healthy, cleanly-configured cluster. Pre-2.0, ops teams saw replica fetchers crashing with out-of-order offset errors or, worse, nothing at all while replicas quietly disagreed.
- **Kubernetes / cloud churn:** frequent pod reschedules, broker restarts, and controller-driven leadership moves multiply "fast consecutive failovers," the precondition for both scenarios.
- **Aggressive availability configs (RF=2, min.isr=1, unclean election enabled):** IoT/metrics/logging pipelines that prefer uptime over durability get Scenario 2's alternating-survivor pattern during rolling outages; KIP-279 guarantees the replicas at least converge again instead of diverging forever.

**Where the guarantee is load-bearing today:**

- **Replica consistency as a product guarantee:** "all in-sync replicas contain identical committed data" underpins consumer correctness (any replica can be read after failover — including modern follower fetching), changelog restoration for Kafka Streams state stores, and CDC consumers rebuilding caches/search indexes. Silent divergence would poison all of them; KIP-279 is what rules it out under leadership churn.
- **Financial/event-sourced systems:** for an order/payments pipeline, the n==20 silent case is the nightmare scenario — two replicas replaying *different* order events at the same offsets to different consumers. Post-2.0 that can't happen with clean election.
- **KRaft (KIP-595):** the metadata quorum's fetch-time divergence check ("if the fetcher's last epoch doesn't match the epoch of the offset before the fetch offset, truncate") is the evolved, per-fetch form of KIP-279's reconciliation — cluster metadata integrity inherits this work.
- **As a design pattern:** any leader-based replicated log you build (WAL shipping, a replicated state machine, even a multi-region ledger service) needs (a) generation-stamped entries, (b) self-describing reconciliation responses, and (c) an iterative walk-back to the common prefix. KIP-279 is a compact real-world case study of why (b) and (c) are non-optional — a strong senior-interview artifact for "how do replicas re-sync after failover?"

## 7. TL;DR

KIP-101's single-round epoch truncation still diverged when the follower's newest epoch was unknown to the leader — reachable via a routine **preferred leader election + immediate second failover** (clean!) or alternating sole-survivor restarts (unclean). Depending on batch alignment this caused runtime fetch errors or **silent same-offset-different-data divergence**. KIP-279 (Kafka 2.0) adds the **leader epoch to the OffsetsForLeaderEpoch response** and makes the follower **iteratively walk back through epoch history** until leader and follower find their largest common epoch, then truncate to the largest common offset in it — i.e., the true largest common log prefix, with a one-round-trip guarantee for clean elections and an HW fallback when history is exhausted. It completes Kafka's answer to the log-reconciliation problem Raft solves with (term, index) matching, and it's the reason replica logs provably converge even under rapid leadership churn.
